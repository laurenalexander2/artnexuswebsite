Art Nexus

Semantic search for museum collections — how humanity will access its archives.

What we’re building

Today, art collections are the world’s largest unstructured knowledge base, trapped in silos that don’t talk to each other. Art Nexus is the missing infrastructure: a semantic layer that lets collections think together.

We ingest collection records and media, generate domain-trained multimodal embeddings (text + image + metadata), and map that information into an evolving cultural knowledge graph. Objects become discoverable by meaning, relationships, iconography, provenance, technique, style, or conceptual connection—not just keyword matches.

By plotting in vector space a digital twin of every known art piece, this is the interface to humanity’s archives.

For institutions

Art Nexus sits on top of existing collection systems and turns static records into a living, searchable network. Instead of brittle keyword fields and siloed databases, curators and registrars get a semantic map of their holdings— and, eventually, of the world’s collections.

  • Unify siloed databases into a single semantic layer.
  • Search by concept: “surrealist female portrait, 1950s, lithograph” — not exact field matches.
  • Reveal latent connections: shared iconography, technical affinities, workshop lineages, provenance trails.
  • Support research, exhibitions, and loans with cross-collection discovery.

Art Nexus is designed as an institutional discovery layer and a foundation for future integrations with vector databases, vocabularies, and curator-facing tools.

For users

For researchers, students, and everyday visitors, Art Nexus feels like a search engine built for art, not for the web. You describe what you’re looking for in natural language, and the system returns meaning-linked neighbors across collections.

  • Search by feeling, motif, technique, or narrative instead of object IDs and accession numbers.
  • Follow visual and conceptual “threads” through time, place, and medium.
  • Surface overlooked objects that sit just outside canonical labels or cataloging vocabularies.
  • Use similarity search to explore “more like this” across institutions, not just within one museum.

The goal is an interface where someone curious about “early feminist printmaking,” “diasporic surrealism,” or “ritual bronze vessels with animal handles” can step directly into the relevant constellation of works.

The prototype

The current prototype ingests collection exports (CSV + image hierarchy), generates embeddings, indexes metadata, and performs semantic retrieval. A query is converted into the same embedding space as the objects, and the system returns meaning-linked similarity results across the dataset.

In the demo, a museum can upload a Met-style open-access CSV, watch the index build in batches, and immediately run semantic searches over hundreds of thousands of objects—no schema rewrites, no keyword engineering.
